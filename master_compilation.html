What is the Higgs Boson?
The Higgs boson is a fundamental particle that provides evidence for the Higgs field, which gives mass to other particles in the universe.

⚛️ Fundamental Particle
The Higgs boson is an elementary particle in the Standard Model of particle physics. Unlike other particles, it has zero spin and is the only scalar boson known to exist.

🌌 Mass Generation
The Higgs field permeates all of space and gives mass to particles through their interaction with this field. The Higgs boson is the quantum excitation of this field.

🔬 Experimental Proof
Discovered at CERN's Large Hadron Collider in 2012, the Higgs boson's detection confirmed the Standard Model's predictions about mass generation.

🏆 Nobel Prize
Peter Higgs and François Englert received the 2013 Nobel Prize in Physics for theoretically predicting the Higgs mechanism in 1964.

Key Research Note: The Higgs boson's discovery was one of the most significant scientific achievements of the 21st century, validating decades of theoretical work and completing the Standard Model of particle physics.

The Higgs Mechanism
The Higgs mechanism explains how particles acquire mass through spontaneous symmetry breaking. In the early universe, particles were massless and traveled at the speed of light. As the universe cooled, the Higgs field developed a non-zero vacuum expectation value, breaking electroweak symmetry and giving mass to particles that interact with this field.

• Vacuum State: The Higgs field has a non-zero value even in empty space, creating a "Mexican hat" potential.
• Coupling Strength: Different particles have different coupling strengths to the Higgs field, determining their masses.
• Gauge Invariance: The mechanism preserves gauge invariance while allowing for mass generation.
