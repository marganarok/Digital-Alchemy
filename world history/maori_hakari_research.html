Overview: The Māori of Aotearoa

The Māori are the indigenous Polynesian people of mainland New Zealand (Aotearoa). They are the second-largest ethnic group in New Zealand, after European New Zealanders (commonly known by the Māori name Pākehā). The Māori arrived in New Zealand from eastern Polynesia, settling the islands between approximately 1320 and 1350 CE during what is known as the Great Migration.

Understanding Hakari: The Sacred Feast

hakari
/ˈhäkərē/
noun • New Zealand English
Definition: A Māori feast that follows a funeral ceremony or marks another important occasion.
"Such large quantities of food are prepared that some hakaris last for several weeks"

Cultural Significance

The hakari represents far more than a simple meal—it is a fundamental expression of manaakitanga (hospitality), whanaungatanga (kinship), and utu (reciprocity). These feasts serve as critical social institutions that reinforce tribal bonds, settle disputes, celebrate achievements, and honor the deceased.

Traditional Occasions

  • Tangihanga (funeral ceremonies)
  • Weddings and births
  • Treaty signings
  • Tribal gatherings
  • War victories
  • House openings (whare whakairo)

Core Values Expressed

  • Manaakitanga (hospitality)
  • Whanaungatanga (kinship)
  • Utu (reciprocity)
  • Mana (prestige/authority)
  • Kaitiakitanga (guardianship)
  • Aroha (love/compassion)

Historical Context

1320-1350 CE
The Great Migration: Māori ancestors arrive in Aotearoa from eastern Polynesia in waka (ocean-going canoes). They bring with them the tradition of communal feasting that would evolve into the hakari.
Pre-1840
Pre-European Era: Hakari serves as crucial political and social institution. Large feasts demonstrate tribal mana and wealth, with some lasting weeks and feeding thousands of guests.
1840
Treaty of Waitangi: Signed between Māori chiefs and British Crown. Hakari plays role in bringing tribes together for this historic moment.
1860s-1900s
Colonial Suppression: Many Māori cultural practices, including large-scale hakari, are discouraged or banned under assimilation policies.
1970s-Present
Cultural Renaissance: Māori cultural revival brings renewed emphasis on traditional practices including hakari. Modern hakari blend traditional values with contemporary New Zealand culture.

Preparation and Logistics

Preparing for a major hakari was a massive undertaking requiring months of preparation:
  • Food Gathering: Hunting parties, fishing expeditions, and cultivation of crops (kumara, taro) began months in advance
  • Storage Systems: Elaborate pātaka (elevated storehouses) were built to preserve food
  • Cooking Facilities: Large hāngi pits were dug, capable of cooking hundreds of kilograms of food simultaneously
  • Labor Organization: Entire communities were mobilized, with specific roles assigned by age and gender
  • Guest Accommodations: Temporary shelters and meeting houses were constructed

Traditional Hakari Foods

Seafood (Kai Moana)

New Zealand's abundant coastal waters provided the foundation of hakari feasts:
  • Kina (sea urchin)
  • Pāua (abalone)
  • Kōura (crayfish)
  • Numerous fish species including snapper, kahawai, and trevally
  • Tio (oysters)
  • Pipi and tuatua (shellfish)

Land-Based Foods

  • Kūmara: Sweet potato, a staple crop brought from Polynesia
  • Taro: Another Polynesian import, grown in warmer regions
  • Fernroot (aruhe): Processed from bracken fern, a carbohydrate source
  • Birds: Including kererū (wood pigeon), tūī, and coastal seabirds
  • Kiore: Pacific rat, considered a delicacy

Modern Additions

Contemporary hakari blend traditional foods with introduced ingredients:
  • Pork and mutton (introduced by Europeans)
  • Modern vegetables and salads
  • Bread and wheat-based foods
  • Desserts and cakes
  • Beverages including tea and coffee

Social and Political Functions

The hakari served multiple critical functions in Māori society beyond mere sustenance:

1. Demonstration of Mana

The scale and quality of a hakari directly reflected the mana (prestige, power, authority) of the hosting chief and tribe. A successful hakari enhanced tribal status and political influence.

2. Diplomatic Platform

Hakari provided neutral ground for negotiations between tribes, peace treaties, and alliance formations. The act of sharing food created bonds of reciprocity and mutual obligation.

3. Economic Exchange

Feasts facilitated trade networks, with guests bringing gifts (koha) and establishing economic relationships that extended beyond the event.

4. Cultural Transmission

Hakari served as educational venues where oral histories, genealogies (whakapapa), and cultural knowledge were formally transmitted to younger generations.
"Nau te rourou, naku te rourou, ka ora ai te iwi" — With your food basket and my food basket, the people will thrive

Contemporary Hakari Practice

Today, hakari continues as a living tradition, adapted to modern New Zealand society while retaining core cultural values:

Modern Context

  • Marae-based: Most hakari now occur at marae (tribal meeting grounds) with established kitchens and dining facilities
  • Tangihanga Central: Funeral feasts remain the most common form of hakari, with whānau (extended family) feeding mourners for 3-5 days
  • Celebration Events: Weddings, birthdays, and anniversaries incorporate hakari principles
  • Political Gatherings: Government consultations and treaty negotiations often include formal hakari
  • Tourism Integration: Some marae offer cultural experiences including traditional feasts

Challenges and Adaptations

Modern hakari face several challenges:
  • Cost pressures in preparing food for large groups
  • Time constraints in contemporary work schedules
  • Environmental concerns around overfishing and sustainability
  • Health and safety regulations around food preparation
  • Balancing tradition with practical modern needs
Despite these challenges, hakari remains a vital expression of Māori identity and continues to evolve while maintaining its essential cultural significance.
