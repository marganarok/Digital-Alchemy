Foundation & Early History

"We must teach firmly and be gentle and humble with our students, treating them as our Lord treated His apostles." ‚Äî Saint Madeleine Sophie Barat
November 21, 1800
Foundation in Amiens
Madeleine Sophie Barat, along with three companions, established the Society of the Sacred Heart in Amiens, France. The foundation came during the tumultuous period following the French Revolution.
1801-1804
First Educational Ventures
The first Sacred Heart school opened in Amiens, focusing on education for young women. The curriculum combined religious formation with academic excellence, revolutionary for its time.
1806
Papal Recognition
Pope Pius VII granted preliminary approval to the Society's constitutions. This pontifical recognition established the order's legitimacy within the Catholic Church.
1815
Paris Expansion
The Society established its first school in Paris, marking the beginning of urban educational ministry. This expansion attracted students from aristocratic and wealthy merchant families.
1826
Final Papal Approval
Pope Leo XII granted definitive approval to the Society's constitutions, making it a Pontifical Right religious institute.

Global Expansion Timeline

1818
First International Expansion
Sacred Heart sisters arrived in Louisiana, United States. Mother Rose Philippine Duchesne led this pioneering mission to the American frontier.
1820s-1830s
European Growth
Schools established in Italy, Austria, Belgium, and Switzerland. The Society adapted to different cultural contexts while maintaining educational standards.
1842

First Sacred Heart school opened in Chile, beginning the Society's significant presence in Latin America.
1860s

Schools founded in Ireland and England, serving both Catholic and Protestant families seeking quality education.
1880s-1890s
Asian Expansion
Sacred Heart education reached Japan and the Philippines, adapting Western pedagogical methods to Asian contexts.
1900s-1920s
African and Oceanic Presence
Schools established in Australia, New Zealand, and various African countries, completing the Society's global reach.
1950s-Present

Society rebuilt European institutions and adapted to Vatican II reforms, embracing contemporary educational methods.
